Winkler Puzzle 2: Subsets of 1-100

2025/05/22

Here’s another Peter Winkler puzzle:

Prove that any set of 10 integers between 1 and 100 has two disjoint non-empty subsets with the same sum

Here’s a hint:

Show hint Use the pigeonhole principle

And the solution:

Show solution The largest sum any 10 integers less than or equal 100 could have is 950 (=5*190). Therefore, there are at most 950 distinct sums in the subset.

How many ways are there to divide 10 integers into two disjoint non-empty subsets? Well, it’s the number of ternary strings of length 10, minus the number which don’t have at least one 1 and one 2 - in other words, it’s the number of ternary strings of length 10 minus the number of binary strings of length 10, divided by two: $(3^{10} - 2^{10})/2$, which is much greater than 950 (why divided by two? because we consider that just swapping the contents of each set doesn’t matter for our counting). Since there are more ways to generate sums than there are possible sums to generate, at least one sum is repeated.

But if you don’t believe me (I didn’t bother to calculate it), there’s a way to get the answer using just mental math: consider the number of ways to generate pairs of exhaustive non-empty disjoint subsets; this is just the number binary strings of length 10, minus two (for the all-zeros and all-ones strings that produce an empty subset). $2^{10}-2 = 1022$, which is also greater than 950. So even a relaxation of this problem still leaves us with a comfortable margin.
